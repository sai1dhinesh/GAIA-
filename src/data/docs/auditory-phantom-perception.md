# Auditory Phantom Perception: Tinnitus as a Unified Percept
## An integrative model of interacting separable subnetworks

**Authors**: Dirk De Ridder, Sven Vanneste, Nathan Weisz, Alain Londero, Winnie Schlee, Ana Belen Elgoyhen, Berthold Langguth
**Published**: Neuroscience and Biobehavioral Reviews 44 (2014) 16–32

### Abstract
Tinnitus is considered to be an auditory phantom phenomenon, a persistent conscious percept of a salient memory trace, externally attributed, in the absence of a sound source. It is perceived as a phenomenological unified coherent percept, binding multiple separable clinical characteristics, such as its loudness, the sidedness, the type (pure tone, noise), the associated distress and so on.

### Key Concepts
- **Tinnitus Core**: The minimal set of brain areas that needs to be jointly activated for tinnitus to be consciously perceived, devoid of its affective components.
- **Deafferentation**: Auditory deafferentation limits the amount of information the brain can acquire, increasing uncertainty. The brain actively fills in missing information to reduce free energy.
- **Noise Canceling**: A dysfunctional noise suppressing mechanism could be limbically driven, leading to the loudness percept.
- **Subnetworks**: Phenomenological unified percept of tinnitus is an emergent property of multiple, parallel, dynamically changing and partially overlapping subnetworks.

### Heuristic Framework
The model integrates the tinnitus perceptual core with other related aspects (distress, mood, lateralization). Communication between different subnetworks is proposed to occur at **hubs**—brain areas involved in multiple subnetworks simultaneously—at discrete oscillatory frequencies.

### Clinical Implications
Selectively interfering with one frequency of a specific subnetwork (e.g., sound lateralization via gamma band stimulation or distress via alpha stimulation) could theoretically modulate tinnitus without disrupting other networks.
